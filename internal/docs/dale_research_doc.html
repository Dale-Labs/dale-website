Preamble

The Original Question

DALE did not begin as a technology project.

It began with an observation.

Communities create value every day. Farmers, social entrepreneurs, citizen groups, local organizations, fellows, cooperatives, and informal networks continuously generate knowledge, relationships, commitments, and solutions.

Yet as value moves through larger systems, it becomes increasingly difficult to see.

The original question behind Kuza was simple:

How do we ensure that value created at the edge remains visible as it moves through a system

At the time this appeared in discussions about climate finance, grassroots organizations, women and youth enterprises, and market access. The intuition was that communities were creating value, but the evidence of that value was not surviving the journey.

The Pattern

Over time DALE expanded into multiple validation environments. Each appeared different on the surface. Together they revealed a consistent pattern.

In every environment the challenge was not creation. The challenge was survival.

Observations were created. Relationships were created. Commitments were created. Knowledge was created. Value was created.

The question was whether those records remained meaningful after people, organizations, projects, funding streams, and institutions changed.

The answer was not consistent. And that inconsistency was the finding.

The adversary was not technical failure. The adversary was institutional impermanence.

Organizations close. Projects end. Funding shifts. Staff leave. Governments change. Platforms disappear.

The Hypothesis

DALE tests whether observations, value, commitments, relationships, and context can survive institutional change without losing meaning.

Different environments stress-test different dimensions of survival. Together they examine the same underlying question from different angles.

Part 1: Validation Environments

What a Validation Environment Is

A validation environment is not a DALE product.

It is not a module, a tool, or an application.

A validation environment is a real-world context in which provenance is generated and survival conditions can be observed.

Validation environments exist independently of DALE. DALE observes within them. The environments generate evidence. DALE tools interpret that evidence.

Each environment tests a specific dimension of the central hypothesis. Together they build a cumulative evidence base for provenance survival across different types of institutional change.

Jitambulishe is a civic observability environment. Citizens, community members, and local actors generate observations about conditions, problems, solutions, and events in their immediate context.

Can community observations remain interpretable and useful after the original observers, platforms, and institutions have changed

Part 2: DALE Provenance Core

What the Core Does

The DALE Provenance Core is not a database. It is not a ledger. It is not a dashboard.

It is the structural layer that makes provenance portable.

The core does three things:

It receives records from validation environments.

It structures those records so they remain interpretable across contexts.

It preserves originator relationships so that every record retains a verifiable connection to the actor, community, or institution that generated it.

The core does not own the records it holds. It preserves the relationships between records and their origins. DALE preserves who originated what, who verified what, who transformed what, and who consumed what — without any single actor owning the graph itself.

What the Core Does Not Do

The core does not generate insights directly. It does not produce compliance reports. It does not manage projects or tasks. It does not replace existing traceability, governance, or civic platforms.

Those are functions of DALE Tools and Adapters, which sit downstream of the core.

The moment the core begins optimizing for a specific downstream consumer, it loses universality. The canon structure described below protects against this by keeping the core focused on describing reality rather than satisfying any particular read layer.

The Canon Structure

Canons are the structural vocabularies of the DALE Provenance Core. A canon describes a domain of reality. It defines the objects, relationships, and events that exist within that domain in a way that is precise enough to be machine-readable and general enough to remain valid across contexts.

Canons are not adapters. They do not translate DALE records into external formats. They describe what happened. Adapters translate those descriptions into what specific external systems need to see.

Observation Canon

Describes the structure of observations generated within validation environments.

Core objects: Observer, Observation, Context, Evidence, Verification Status, Relationship Links.

Primary environments: Jitambulishe, JOOUST verification layer within APE.

Exchange Canon

Describes the structure of value exchanges between actors.

Core objects: Actor A, Actor B, Exchange Event, Value Type, Terms, Evidence, Outcome.

Primary environments: OYW SVE, Jielekeze, APE.

Provenance Canon

Describes the structure of provenance chains — the sequence of custody, transformation, and verification events through which a record or asset moves.

Core objects: Origin, Custodian, Transformation Event, Verification Event, Transfer Event, Chain of Custody.

Primary environments: APE, Jielekeze, OYW SVE.

CBC Canon

Describes the structure of commitments made between stakeholders and the evidence of whether those commitments were honored, adapted, or lost.

Core objects: Commitment Maker, Commitment Receiver, Commitment Terms, Commitment Event, Adaptation Event, Outcome, Evidence.

Primary environments: OYW SVE, BAN, Jielekeze, Jitambulishe.

Supply Chain Canon (SCC)

Describes the structure of physical assets moving through production and distribution chains. The SCC is a connector canon that adds the physical asset layer agricultural supply chains require.

The Provenance Graph

The provenance graph is the accumulated record of all observations, exchanges, commitments, and custody events across all validation environments.

It has three distinguishing properties:

It preserves originator relationships. Every record retains a verifiable connection to the actor who generated it.

It is cross-environment. Records from Jitambulishe and APE can be connected without either environment needing to know about the other.

It survives institutional change. The graph does not depend on any single organization, platform, or funding stream for its integrity.

Provenance as Institutional Memory

DALE provenance functions as institutional memory.

Not memory owned by a single institution.

Memory that survives movement between institutions.

Most institutional memory fails because it is stored inside the institution that created it. When the institution changes, the memory degrades or disappears. DALE provenance is stored in relationship to its origins rather than inside any single institutional container.

Part 3: DALE Tools

What DALE Tools Are

DALE Tools are interpretation engines. They are not validation environments. They do not generate provenance records. They do not originate evidence.

DALE Tools receive provenance records from validation environments and apply structured interpretation frameworks to answer specific questions about what those records mean.

A validation environment answers: did the provenance survive A DALE Tool answers: what does the provenance mean

Tool Summary

ToolPurposePrimary Input
State ReviewOrigin state captureAll environments
CoSContext interpretationJitambulishe, Jielekeze, APE
SECAdaptation interpretationJielekeze, OYW SVE
CBCCommitment interpretationAll environments

Tool 1: State Review

Purpose: Origin state capture.

State Review is the entry point into the DALE interpretation layer. It captures the condition of an actor, community, organization, or system at a defined point in time — establishing a baseline against which subsequent observations, exchanges, and outcomes can be measured.

Key question answered: What was the condition at origin, and what has changed since

Tool 2: Context of Systems (CoS)

Purpose: Context interpretation.

CoS interprets the systemic context surrounding provenance records. It identifies the relationships, conditions, pressures, and environmental factors that shaped what was observed, exchanged, or committed to within a validation environment.

CoS answers the question that most data systems ignore: not just what happened, but why the conditions existed for it to happen.

Key question answered: What systemic conditions shaped this provenance record, and how do those conditions affect its meaning

Tool 3: Systems Evolution Canvas (SEC)

Purpose: Adaptation interpretation.

SEC interprets how actors, systems, and value pathways have adapted over time in response to changing conditions. Where CoS captures the context at a point in time, SEC captures the trajectory — how conditions, relationships, and value pathways have changed across the provenance record.

Key question answered: How has this actor, system, or value pathway adapted over time, and does that adaptation represent continuity or degradation

Tool 4: Community Benefit Commitment (CBC)

Purpose: Commitment interpretation.

CBC interprets commitment provenance. It receives records of what was promised, by whom, to whom, under what conditions — and tracks whether those commitments were honored, adapted, or lost as circumstances changed.

CBC is unique among DALE Tools in that it captures intention as well as action. CBC does not generate commitments. It interprets commitment records that originate within validation environments.

Key question answered: What was promised, what happened, and did the commitment survive institutional change

Tool 5: Insights

Purpose: Cross-environment analysis.

Insights is the cross-environment observability layer. It receives interpreted records from all other DALE Tools and identifies patterns, connections, and signals that are only visible when provenance records from multiple environments are held in relationship to each other.

Insights is where the provenance graph becomes analytically useful. The blocked irrigation canal observed in Jitambulishe and the 20 percent yield reduction recorded in Jielekeze are separate records. Insights connects them.

Key question answered: What patterns emerge when provenance records from multiple environments are held in relationship to each other

Part 4: Adapters

What Adapters Are

Adapters are read layers. They translate DALE provenance records into formats, vocabularies, and structures that external systems require.

Adapters do not generate provenance. They do not interpret provenance. They consume it.

This distinction is the most important governance boundary in the DALE architecture. The moment the provenance core begins optimizing for a specific adapter, the core loses universality. Adapters exist so that the core never has to make that compromise.

What Adapters Are Not

Adapters are not products. They are not the reason DALE exists.

The commercial opportunity in adapters is real. But the moment adapter requirements begin driving core architecture decisions, DALE stops being provenance infrastructure and becomes a compliance tool, a carbon tool, or a governance tool.

The adapters serve the core. The core does not serve the adapters.

Part 5: Validation Lifecycle

What the Validation Lifecycle Is

The validation lifecycle is the progression through which the central DALE hypothesis is tested. It is not a product roadmap. It is not a funding timeline. It is a research architecture.

StageQuestion
OriginCan communities, producers, and stakeholders generate DALE-native provenance records
SurviveCan those records remain interpretable, verifiable, and useful as they move across institutional boundaries
CommitCan commitments embedded in provenance records survive personnel turnover, organizational change, and adaptation
ConsumeCan external systems consume DALE provenance without recreating it

Why the Sequence Matters

Each stage has a dependency. Survival cannot be tested until origination is confirmed. Commitment cannot be tested until survival is confirmed. Consumption cannot be tested until commitment is confirmed.

The sequence reflects the logical structure of the provenance survival hypothesis itself.

Stage 2: Survive

Can provenance records remain interpretable, verifiable, and useful as they move across institutional boundaries

This is the central unanswered question in the DALE validation architecture. Stage 1 is largely evidenced. Stage 2 is the threshold the entire architecture is currently designed to cross.

Three criteria apply to every survival test event:

Interpretable — can an actor who did not generate the record understand what it means without requiring the originator to explain it

Verifiable — can an actor who did not generate the record confirm that what it claims is accurate

Useful — can an actor who did not generate the record act on it without recreating it from scratch

Validation Priorities

Immediate Priority — Stage 2 Survival Testing in APE

The minimum viable survival test — one producer record across three node transfers at Samaky Hub — is the highest leverage validation event currently available. Everything downstream of this test becomes significantly more evidenced if it confirms the survival hypothesis.

Concurrent Priority — Jielekeze Activation

Jielekeze is the only proposed environment that feeds multiple stages simultaneously. It generates origination evidence for Stage 1, value and continuity records for Stage 2, producer commitments for Stage 3, and carbon and community benefit inputs for Stage 4.

Medium Term Priority — Structured Commitment Survival Tests

OYW SVE and BAN both have sufficient provenance records to design structured commitment survival tests. Identifying a specific commitment, tracking it across a defined transition event, and measuring whether it remains interpretable, verifiable, and useful after the transition would provide Stage 3 evidence without requiring new environment activation.

Longer Term Priority — Compliance Consumption Testing via APE

Once APE generates survival evidence, the Compliance Adapter becomes testable. Engaging a compliance buyer — an exporter or processor facing EUDR requirements — to attempt to satisfy due diligence using APE-generated provenance records would provide the first Stage 4 consumption evidence and the first direct commercial validation of the revenue architecture.

Appendix A: DALE Origin Statement

Reproduced in full as the founding document of the DALE validation architecture.

The Original Question

DALE did not begin as a technology project. It began with an observation.

Communities create value every day. Farmers, social entrepreneurs, citizen groups, local organizations, fellows, cooperatives, and informal networks continuously generate knowledge, relationships, commitments, and solutions.

Yet as value moves through larger systems, it often becomes increasingly difficult to see.

How do we ensure that value created at the edge remains visible as it moves through a system

What We Learned

Over time DALE expanded into multiple validation environments. Each appeared different on the surface. Together they revealed a consistent pattern.

In every environment the challenge was not creation. The challenge was survival. The answer was not consistent. And that inconsistency was the finding.

The Pattern

The adversary is not technical failure. The adversary is institutional impermanence.

Organizations close. Projects end. Funding shifts. Staff leave. Governments change. Platforms disappear. The central question becomes: what survives

The DALE Hypothesis

DALE tests whether observations, value, commitments, relationships, and context can survive institutional change without losing meaning.

Provenance as Institutional Memory

DALE is not primarily a governance platform, a compliance platform, or a civic participation platform. DALE is a provenance infrastructure.

Provenance is the ability to understand what happened, in context, with evidence, after the original circumstances have changed.

In this sense provenance functions as institutional memory. Not memory owned by a single institution. Memory that survives movement between institutions.

The Validation Lifecycle

Origin — Can communities, producers, and stakeholders generate records

Survive — Can those records remain interpretable, verifiable, and useful as they move

Commit — Can commitments remain visible through adaptation and turnover

Consume — Can external systems use provenance without recreating it

The Purpose of DALE

DALE exists to preserve the provenance of what communities, producers, and stakeholders create so that value remains visible, meaningful, and usable long after the systems around it have changed.

Appendix D: Glossary

Adapter A translation layer that maps DALE provenance records into formats required by external systems. Adapters consume provenance. They do not generate it. Current adapters: Governance, Compliance, Carbon, Community Benefit.

Agricultural Provenance Environment (APE) The proposed DALE validation environment testing whether provenance can survive movement across organizational boundaries in a market-driven agricultural context. Primary nodes: Samaky Hub, JOOUST, PSA. Status: Proposed.

BAN Active DALE validation environment testing relationship provenance survival across funding cycles, organizational transitions, and leadership change.

Canon A structured vocabulary describing a domain of reality within the DALE Provenance Core. Canons describe what happened. They do not translate records for external consumption. Current canons: Observation, Exchange, Provenance, CBC, Supply Chain.

CBC (Community Benefit Commitment) Both a DALE canon and a DALE Tool. As a canon: the structured vocabulary for recording commitments, adaptations, and outcomes. As a tool: the interpretation engine that tracks whether commitments survive institutional change.

Chain of Custody The complete sequence of custodians and transfer events through which a physical asset or provenance record has moved.

CoS (Context of Systems) DALE Tool for interpreting the systemic context surrounding provenance records.

Consumption Stage 4 of the DALE Validation Lifecycle. Tests whether external systems can use DALE provenance without recreating it.

Context Provenance Provenance type generated by Jitambulishe. Records of what was observed, by whom, in what circumstances, with what evidence.

Commitment Provenance Provenance type generated through CBC across all environments. Records of what was promised, what happened, and whether the commitment survived institutional change.

Exchange Provenance Provenance type generated by OYW SVE. Records of what moved between actors, under what terms, with what evidence and outcome.

Institutional Impermanence The primary adversary tested by the DALE validation architecture. The condition in which organizations close, projects end, funding shifts, staff leave, and platforms disappear — causing provenance records to degrade or disappear with them.

Institutional Memory The ability of a system to remember what happened after the people who witnessed it have moved on. DALE provenance functions as institutional memory that survives movement between institutions.

Insights DALE Tool for cross-environment analysis. Receives interpreted records from all other tools and identifies patterns visible only when provenance from multiple environments is held in relationship.

Jielekeze Proposed DALE validation environment testing value and continuity provenance survival. Native unit: producer or enterprise journey. Status: Proposed.

Jitambulishe Active DALE validation environment testing context provenance survival. Native unit: citizen observation. Status: Active.

JOOUST Jaramogi Oginga Odinga University of Science and Technology. Academic coordination and verification node within APE.

Kuza The original project from which DALE emerged. Kuza identified the central question that DALE now tests: how do we ensure that value created at the edge remains visible as it moves through a system

Observation The foundational unit of DALE provenance. Every provenance type begins with an observation of some kind.

Origin Stage 1 of the DALE Validation Lifecycle. Tests whether communities, producers, and stakeholders can generate consistent DALE-native provenance records.

OYW SVE Active DALE validation environment testing exchange provenance survival across geographic, organizational, and cultural boundaries within the One Young World East Africa network. Status: Active.

Provenance The ability to understand what happened, in context, with evidence, after the original circumstances have changed. DALE’s core function is preserving provenance across institutional change.

Provenance Core The structural layer of DALE that receives records from validation environments, structures them using canons, and preserves originator relationships within the provenance graph.

Provenance Graph The accumulated connected structure of all observations, exchanges, commitments, and custody events across all DALE validation environments. Preserves originator relationships. Survives institutional change. Cross-environment by design.

Provenance Portability The property of a provenance record that allows it to remain interpretable, verifiable, and useful when accessed by an actor or system that was not present at origination. The primary hypothesis tested by APE.

PSA (Pamela Steele Associates) Supply chain expertise node within APE. Brings operational supply chain systems knowledge from health supply chain contexts.

Relationship Provenance Provenance type generated by BAN. Records of who is connected to whom, through what history, with what evidence of interaction across organizational and funding transitions.

Samaky Hub Community-level producer node within APE. Primary record origination site for APE. Observes fish value chains, producer activity, cooperative formation, market interactions, and community benefit creation.

SEC (Systems Evolution Canvas) DALE Tool for adaptation interpretation. Answers: how has this actor, system, or value pathway adapted over time, and does that adaptation represent continuity or degradation

State Review DALE Tool for origin state capture. Establishes the baseline condition against which subsequent observations, exchanges, and outcomes are measured.

Survive Stage 2 of the DALE Validation Lifecycle. The central unanswered hypothesis. Tests whether provenance records remain interpretable, verifiable, and useful as they move across institutional boundaries.

Validation Environment A real-world context in which provenance is generated and survival conditions can be observed. Validation environments exist independently of DALE. They generate evidence. DALE tools interpret that evidence.

Validation Lifecycle The four-stage research architecture through which the DALE central hypothesis is tested: Origin → Survive → Commit → Consume.

Value Provenance Provenance type generated by Jielekeze. Records of what was produced, by whom, through what process, exchanged with whom, under what conditions, with what outcome.
